Calum Ross

From 28th September 2020 I am based at University College Cork as a lecturer in physics. The post lasts 12 months and in the first semester I will be teaching PY2101 Classical Mechanics.

I am a postdoctoral researcher in the Topological Sciences group at Keio university.

My research interests relate to Topological Solitons and their links to geometry, Differential Geometry and Gauge Theory. In particular my PhD work focused on studying a geometric interpretation of integrable vortex equations. More recently I have become interested in magnetic skyrmions. Currently I am studying a toy model for magnetic skyrmions which has exact solutions.

I did my PhD student in the mathematical physics group at Heriot-Watt University under the supervison of Bernd Schroers. While in Edinburgh I was a member of the Edinburgh Mathematical Physics Group. I have an MPhys in Theoretical Physics from Lancaster University and a MASt in Applied Mathematics from the University of Cambridge. At Lancaster I wrote a Master's project on Physical Applications of Products of Geometric Distributions and while at Cambridge I wrote a Part III Essay on the Geometry of Supersymmetric and Super-Conformal Quantum Mechanics.

For the academic years 2016-2017 and 2017-2018 I organised the PhD student seminar series here at Heriot-Watt. This is a weekly informal seminar series held in CM/T.01 on Fridays, offering PhD students to present their work or a topic that they are interested in to their peers. For the academic year 2018-2019 Alex Evetts has taken over organising them. The talks are sometimes advertised here and a record of previous talks as well as upcoming talks is here.

Between January and September 2017 I participated in a reading group on String Theory and D-Branes.

From October 2017 until January 2018 there was a reading group running on renormalisation and effective field theories. Following this reading group we decided to start a short seminar series, the details of which are here.

In October 2018 we started a reading group on statistical field theory which ran until March 2019.
